Interleukin Genetics' (ILIU) CEO Mark Carbeau on Q2 2015 Results - Earnings Call Transcript

Aug. 14, 2015 11:35 AM ET  |  About: Interleukin Genetics, Inc. (ILIU) by: SA Transcripts

Executives

Mark Carbeau - Chief Executive Officer, Director

Kenneth Kornman - President, Chief Scientific Officer, Director

Stephen DiPalma - Interim Chief Financial Officer

Analysts

Brian Marckx - Zacks Investment

Interleukin Genetics Inc (OTCQB:ILIU) Q2 2015 Earnings Conference Call August 14, 2015 8:30 AM ET

Operator

Good morning, ladies and gentlemen. Welcome to the Interleukin Genetics Second Quarter 2015 Earnings call. At this time, all participants are in a listen-only mode. With us today from Interleukin Genetics are Mark Carbeau, Chief Executive Officer; Dr. Kenneth Kornman, President and Chief Scientific Officer; and Stephen DiPalma, Interim Chief Financial Officer.

Please be advised, this call is being recorded at the company's request and it will be archived on the company's website at www.ilgenetics.com. We will be facilitating a question-and-answer session at the end of Management's formal remarks.

At this time, I would like to turn the call over to Mr. DiPalma. Please go ahead, sir.

Stephen DiPalma

Thank you. Good morning, everyone. Thank you for joining us this morning to discuss Interleukin's financial results and corporate highlights for the second quarter ended June 30, 2015.

On today's call Mark Carbeau will first provide some introductory remarks. I will then review the financial results for the second quarter of 2015. Following that Mark will offer some closing remarks and then Ken, Mark and I will be available for the question-and-answer portion of today's call.

Before moving into discussion of the second quarter results and highlights, please note that today's remarks will contain forward-looking statements that involve risks and uncertainties. These risks and uncertainties are outlined in Interleukin's filings with the Securities and Exchange Commission, including its Annual Report on Form 10-K for the year ended December 31, 2014. As such, actual results may differ materially from what is discussed on today's call.

Please note that our financial results release and quarterly report on Form 10-Q was filed with the SEC this morning and are available today on the Investor Relations page of our website, www.ilgenetics.com.

With that, I will turn the call over to Mark Carbeau. Mark?

Mark Carbeau

Thank you, Steve. Good morning, everyone. Thank you for joining us for today's call. At Interleukin Genetics, we continue to advance our mission to prevent and more cost-effectively manage chronic diseases.

As you may recall, I joined the company in April, I have met some of you during these first months. For all shareholders, I look forward to meeting you and keeping you updated on our progress.

I am excited to build on the foundation set by Dr. Ken Kornman, who remains active as our President and Chief Scientific Officer.

Today, I would like to share with you my assessment and plans moving forward. First, our company is founded on solid scientific and is positioned participate in an area of medicine with growing significance. Major serious chronic diseases, including diabetes, cardiovascular disease, osteoarthritis and periodontal disease, all have a substantial inflammatory component that is mediated by the Interlukin-1 or IL-1 pathway.

As the biology of IL-1 became better understood, this pathway has emerged as an important target for biopharmaceutical companies who have several compounds in clinical development that may block IL-1 biologic activity. Our innovative and proprietary technology can identify individuals with lifelong predisposition to overproduce IL-1.

We believe this technology has substantial medical and commercial value across a broad range of diagnostic and therapeutic indications. This premise is backed by both, strong scientific evidence and compelling health economic data and is the foundation upon which we are building our business.

Our major focus to commercialize this technology has been with PerioPredict. For its initial launch, we positioned PerioPredict as a genetics based tool to personalize dental care. This pilot provided two important insights to inform our plan forward. First, we noted that patients and dental professionals see value to the genetic test and are largely comfortable with its use.

Second, while employers are interested in new benefits programs, we noted in difference with the plan design that focuses principally on dental benefits. With America's changing healthcare landscape, most employers are seeking innovation on medical benefits more than on dental or vision.

Also during the past year, we have compiled scientific and compelling health economic data that establishes an association between management and periodontal disease and other chronic diseases mediated by IL-1, such as cardiovascular disease and type-2 diabetes. As a result of these advances and the learnings from our pilot, we have enhanced our commercial positioning for PerioPredict to better highlight its value on the medical side.

PerioPredict powers a disease prevention program that targets individuals with chronic conditions such as type-2 diabetes and cardiovascular disease who will benefit from increased dental preventive care that can substantially lower systemic inflammation in some individuals. This positioning to engage patients at risk for chronic disease provides an important leverage point to improve wellness and lower cost.

The link between dental preventive care and medical savings was documented in a study published in August of 2014, in the Journal of Preventive Medicine that examined 338,000 patients. The study demonstrated that proper dental preventive care directed to the proper patients saves an average of approximately $2,800 per year per patient for a treatment of the diabetic patient, $5, 700 per year for a stroke or cardiovascular disease patient and $1,100 per year for coronary artery patient. These data are very significant. This impacts huge patient groups with very meaningful benefits and is based on a very large study.

We have already begun sharing these compelling data with our target customers, including large insurers and employers and they are increasingly interested in this value proposition. With this enhanced positioning, we have developed and are executing a plan that has two phases to drive PerioPredict sales growth. First phase target sales to employers. The second phase leverages insurance carriers.

As you may recall, our commercial ecosystem involves four key stakeholders, employers as purchases or benefits, insurance carriers, dental professionals and patients

In the first phase, we are targeting large employers to drive adoption, with specific focus on employers who self-insure on medical benefits since they can monetize these savings.

Our current partner, employee benefits consulting group for EBCG has expertise and strong relationships in the U.S. insurance market and has been instrumental in introducing us to large employer groups to discuss how to incorporate PerioPredict disease prevention program into their benefits and wellness offering.

Second phase targets insurance carriers seeking to capture the value of coordination between medical and dental care. This phase is already underway though it involves a longer sales cycle, typically involving a pilot study and a demonstration program before full rollout.

I feel it is worth mentioning that the sales cycle for both phases can be lengthy since most benefit plans trigger off of January 1 enrollment calendar, with plan design decisions often made months in advance. As we have indicated in past calls, we do not anticipate material PerioPredict revenue in 2015.

Nevertheless, we are encouraged by the positive reception of our outreach efforts and we are receiving positive reception from both, large employer groups and insurance carriers and we are working hard to translate this interest into tangible commercial relationships. To support this plan, we are building infrastructure to drive success.

We are actively recruiting for additional commercial expertise in sales personnel and we will add capabilities to manage payor relations. Further, we are seeking selective additions to our benefits consulting partnerships to enhance our reach.

We believe these initiatives will provide a solid foundation and real sales traction for our lead asset and we currently expect to have announceable events occurring on these fronts during the second half of 2015.

Beyond PerioPredict, we are utilizing our two decades of experience in genetic science to develop high-value next-generation genetic tests that leverage our intellectual property and the growing interest in IL-1 biology.

One key area of interest for us is in osteoarthritis or OA, the most common adult joint disease. In July, we executed a license agreement with NYU School of Medicine for the development of a new proprietary genetic test to identify individuals with certain variations in the IL-1 gene cluster and therefore are at risk for OA progression to severe complications.

The test results can be used to predict future severity of the disease and/or treatment decisions, all with the goal of improving patient outcomes. This new OA genetic test is supported by robust scientific data.

Another area where we see significant untapped value is working with pharmaceutical companies to develop companion diagnostics for investigational drug candidates, especially in the area of cardiovascular disease or CVD.

Several companies are testing IL-1 blocking drugs for various CVD related indications, and we believe our proprietary genetic test panels may have value for both, clinicians managing CVD patient care and in guiding the development and use of new drugs that directly or indirectly target IL-1 effects on CVD events. Again, our ambitions are backed by science.

For example, in a recently published study by some Amicus and co-workers from University of California at San Diego clinical studies conducted at the Mayo Clinic demonstrated that information generated by IL-1 genetic tests together with a certain blood lipid are highly predictive of second heart attacks. We believe this test offers significant value to companies developing therapeutics for this patient segment.

The collaboration we announced with Isis Pharmaceuticals earlier this year is a good example of this type of opportunity and an example of what we are exploring with other potential partners in CVD, and we look forward to keeping you informed on future developments.

At this point, I would like to turn the call over to Steve to review our financial results for the second quarter. Steve?

Stephen DiPalma

Thank you, Mark. I hope you have had a chance to review earnings release we issued this morning as well as our quarterly report on Form 10-Q, which we filed with the SEC this morning.

For an overview of the financial results for the quarter ended June 30, 2015. For the quarter, total revenues were $376,000, of which $325,000 was for genetic testing compared to $529,000 of which $483,000 was genetic testing for the same period of 2014, included in genetic testing revenue for the second quarter of 2015 is $52,000 breakage revenue compared to $92,000 of breakage revenue recognized in the second quarter 2014.

As a reminder, we recognize revenue when a test processed in our laboratory and a reported is issued to the customer. Breakage revenue was revenue related to the sales of test kits previously recorded as deferred revenue with the return of the test for processing is considered remote.

Change in total revenue was largely attributable to a decrease in test kits return for processing through access business group's promotional product bundle program.

Cost of revenue for the second quarter of 2015 was $332,000 or 88.2% of total revenue compared to $362,000 or 68.4% of total revenue for the same period in 2014. Increase in cost of revenue as a percent of revenue in the three months ended June 30, 2015 is primarily attributable to the fixed laboratory cost being applied to a lower volume of genetic test being processed during the period.

Research and development expenses for the second quarter of 2015 were $385,000 compared to $216,000 for the second quarter of 2014. This increase is primarily attributable to increased compensation due to annual salary increases for existing staff. Other expenses relating to Ken moving back to R&D from G&A upon Mark joined the company in April 2015.

Selling, general and administrative expenses for the second quarter of 2015 were $1.7 million compared to $1.5 million for the same period in 2014. Increase of approximately $200,000 is primarily due to cost associated recruiting Mark as our new Chief Executive Officer and to higher patent related expenses.

Net loss for the second quarter was $2.1 million or loss of $0.01 per basic and diluted common share compared to net loss of one point $1.6 million or loss of again $0.01 per basic and diluted common share for the same period in 2014.

Cash and cash equivalents were $7.7 million as of June 30, 2015, which we believe is sufficient to fund the company's operations for at least another 12 months.

Now, I would like to turn the call back over to Mark.

Mark Carbeau

Thank you, Steve. Over the coming quarters, we will be executing on a number of initiatives designed to drive the adoption of PerioPredict, which we view as the cornerstone genetic test of power and engaging disease prevention program.

Enhanced commercial positioning for PerioPredict now focuses on patients with high-cost chronic conditions such as cardiovascular disease and type-2 diabetes who will benefit from increased dental care. This enhance positioning is supported by strong scientific and economic health data and is resonating with our key stakeholders.

Personalized wellness is important for patients and employers. Our approach targets the right individuals and drives engagement, thereby serving as a leverage point to better manage these serious high-cost conditions.

Going forward, we will continue to align our value proposition for PerioPredict while maintaining strong linkages to scientific and clinical evidence to succeed in our commercialization efforts we are working to secure select partnerships like EBCG and expand our commercial team where needed. We are also actively pursuing business development opportunities that leverage our genetic expertise in IL-1 intellectual property.

Our near-term focus is on securing partnerships with pharmaceutical companies for the development of companion diagnostics for investigational drug candidates, especially in the area of CVD. There is a large amount of scientific data that supports the IL-1 as a critical regulator a number of chronic diseases and we intend to leverage this strong scientific foundation in driving our business forward.

In closing, we believe our new strategies and objectives are an important step toward maximizing the commercial potential of PerioPredict, an asset that holds tremendous future potential. We are committed to delivering genetic tests and insights that improve individual's lives and support medical professionals.

With the current healthcare paradigm shifting from treatment to prevention, and with our heritage the align science with practical care delivery, we believe, we are well-positioned to be a leader in genetic testing.

We realize there is working had to attain commercial success and we look forward to keeping you updated on our progress throughout the months quarters ahead.

With that, we will be happy to take your questions and I will return the call to the operator.

Question-and-Answer Session

Operator

[Operator Instructions] Our first question comes from Brian Marckx with Zacks Investment.

Brian Marckx

Good morning, guys. Mark, the new insurance plan designs covering PerioPredict, do you envision these as kind of customized for each individual insurance carrier employer group or just more kind of designing a handful of options that they would be choosing from?

Mark Carbeau

Hi, Brian, thanks for the question. It is actually pretty flexible and it is not so much in plan design as it is adaptable to what makes sense for that particular employer. Let me explain that a little bit.

What we are doing is, we are targeting employers who are self-insured on the medical side, if they realize benefits and savings and how these diabetic patients or CVD patients are managed, they can monetize that benefit within their own plan.

We really do not have to modify the medical plan, what we do though is they coordinate with their dental carrier to adjust coverage, so that patients who will benefit from an increased dental preventive care. In fact that will be covered, so they just by adding a rider or some particular vehicle to provide additional dental coverage where indicated, so that is the piece that were having to work on.

It is fairly streamlined. We are not really looking for them to do the actuarial science to adjust based on risk pricing, but rather it is just an administration of additional dental benefit.

Brian Marckx

Okay. With the kind of the interplay between the medical and dental carriers is an envision that potentially the cost of the medical plan may go up and then that may adjust the cost of the dental plan kind of how does that interplay work, I guess.

Mark Carbeau

Yes. We are trying to make it streamlined and simple for the decision process and for implementation. The two additional costs are the costs for the genetic test in this program and the cost of additional dental utilization where that is indicated. That will be on a subset of patients who are indicated for more dental care.

We have discussions with the specific company about how they want to pay for the genetic test. In most cases, they will pay for that as a wellness benefit or through their own programming. They might ask their medical carrier to pay for that is part of their insurance on the medical side, so we see that not occurring too often.

Then on the dental utilization, we do need the dental carrier to provide coverage and reimbursement where additional dental preventive care is needed. What ends up happening is the employer pays a little bit more on the dental size, but they are more than happy to do that because they will generate substantial savings like $2,800 per year and managing that diabetic patients.

The employer is quite happy to make that trade off, and the way the discussion does when we get up to the CFO level is, well, gee, even if you are off by a factor of 10, is a no-brainer. We want to do this. This makes sense and the fact that those savings accrue really in the first year and by the second year, you have already generated substantial savings on the medical side. If I am self-insured and I can monetize that benefit I really want to do this. That is the play that we are experiencing.

Brian Marckx

Okay. Can you talk about the feedback that you have gotten from insurers and employers so far regarding the idea of developing kind of new plans and doing things I guess a little bit different than they have in the past based on bringing on this PerioPredict test into their plans.

Mark Carbeau

Yes. I guess, let me start with the hardest one first and then go to the stuff that is a bit water flowing downhill. Ultimately, this needs to make sense at the clinical level and there is sort of a clinical and assessment or a technical assessment that occurs. Most of these are larger employers, so they are sophistication, they have a Chief Medical Officer or somebody consulting them on benefits designs.

Equivalent there would be the medical directors of the insurance carriers, so those folks are really trying to scrutinized and understand this. I would say some get it. We have heard about dental medical integration, but for the most part people are like, wow I really - I am a little bit skeptical dental intervention can lower systemic inflammation and that has a benefit on managing these other chronic conditions. Well, we get into the science with them and the arguments line up very nicely. The science is very comprehensive and you get to a place that you say yes that makes sense and we have established that in the past.

What starts to happen is, people will say it just feels counterintuitive, so how do we get comfortable with this? Okay, well, let us gets started on your most diseased patients. Let us start with diabetic or maybe diabetic and CVD patients, so that gives us a beachhead into a certain group of patients cohorts, where we know the evidence is very strong and we can start there and build from there.

I would say at the clinical medical trying-to-figure-it-out level, they really want to understand the sciences there to support it and then it gets down to a question of can we make implementation simple and we tend to do that through this beachhead approach.

A little bit easier is just as we go downhill to the employer groups and recall I indicated that is really where we are starting there more than the carriers, the employer groups see this as a place that is a-really-need-wellness-initiative.

I think wellness to the most part has left companies flat-footed with the exception of maybe smoking cessation and they see this as very actionable. This is a dental intervention. I can just get my patient or my member to the dentist office one or two times more per year and that is going to have a benefit on managing CVD risk and managing diabetes health, yes. That seems very straightforward, so it is a very concrete tangible kind of program that we can measure results and major impact very rapidly. At the employee level, there is a lot of interest because it is very actionable.

Brian Marckx

I guess then the big question is, the timeline for when you think some of these plans may be implemented and you mentioned that enrollment is typically in the first of the year. Do you think that potentially we could have some of these plans in the current year for enrollment in early 2016?

Mark Carbeau

I would create two different buckets, again, the employer bucket and the carrier bucket. On the employer bucket, we are working hard to secure some contracts with employers that would result in revenue starting into new plan year, so are our efforts.

I think, we will start to see some of those as demonstration projects that are focused on certain subsets of patient populations and then we will see those expand over time as employers get more comfortable, so that is how I would characterize the employer side.

On the insurance side, what we will do there is, we are in discussions with a number of insurance carriers around setting up pilot programs, so they see the data, they have scrutinized the data, they want to repeat that data in their own population and their own systems, they will do that in a small number of pilot patients with a small number of pilot employers. Then with success, they would do a limited market rollout generally in a geography that is not core to their home turf.

Then with success there, they would roll that out more broadly nationally, so the sales cycle with insurance companies is going to be sort of three phases, but you will start to see some progress with those initial demonstration programs with insurance carriers.

Brian Marckx

Okay. Great. If you could talk about the OA test for a minute, can you talk about what the next steps of development with the test and what role Interleukin will play and what role of NYU School of Medicine will play in development and commercialization?

Kenneth Kornman

Yes. Brian. This is Ken. Thanks for the question. Interleukin will continue to develop that technology and initiate discussions with companies that are engaged in that particular therapeutic space. Our collaborators at NYU are among the world's leaders in this particular disease. We will continue to work with them, they will continue to participate in some way in terms of generating evidence supporting the test and to a great extent it is now in a business development mode and we will move forward with that depending upon interest either pharma companies.

Brian Marckx

Okay. Good. That leads me into the next question. Have you already started discussions with biotech and Pharma companies that might be interested in the technology for clinical trial enrollment purposes?

Mark Carbeau

Are you asking specifically for OA?

Brian Marckx

Yes. For OA.

Mark Carbeau

Yes. We have had a few of those discussions in the past, while we are in early stages of developing the evidence supporting the technology. That entire pharmaceutical area in terms of osteoarthritis has restructured a great deal in the last few years as companies have merged, et cetera, so we will be relooking at a target list of companies to approach relative to that technology. We are not currently engaged in those discussions in OA.

Brian Marckx

Okay. Then one more on potential commercialization of this test in addition to the pharma channel, in your opinion, is there a market for it in clinical diagnosis even though there I am guessing there might be a lack of reimbursement?

Mark Carbeau

Yes. Actually, as we have discussed in the past, our technology fits very nicely with better management in medical practice of some of this chronic disease. Osteoarthritis is a very good example. It is a massive problem. There are no current drugs available to stop the disease progression, so the rheumatologists are really left in a position of how to manage the patients they have.

Beyond the pharmaceutical industry, you have in osteoarthritis a massive medical device industry both, joint replacement, injectables to control pain, a variety of things like that, so we think there is an opportunity worth exploring in terms of direct application to physicians of this technology.

Brian Marckx

Okay. All right. Thanks a lot. That is all I had.

Operator

[Operator Instructions] I am not showing any further questions at this time. I would like to turn the call back over to Mark.

Mark Carbeau

Great. Well, thank you all for participating with us today. We appreciate your continued support in Interleukin Genetics. We look forward to updating you on our progress. Thanks again.

Operator

Ladies and gentlemen, this does conclude today's presentation. You may now disconnect. Have a wonderful day.
