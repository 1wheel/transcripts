Bruce Nardella

Thank you, Dwight, and thanks, everyone, for joining us on today’s call to discuss the results of our fiscal third quarter. We are very pleased with our financial performance in the fiscal third quarter and our continued ability to carry out our long-term growth strategy. Our financial results are highlighted by another quarter of solid growth driven by a strong organic growth in our two largest service lines, I/DD and SRS, as well as what continues to be a very active year for acquisitions.

But most importantly, as we continue to grow, we continue to fulfill our mission by providing high quality cost effective services to more adults and children in need of support. But before we focus on the operational and financial highlights of the quarter, I want to provide a brief update on the state budget and rate environment, which remained very stable in nearly all of our 30, 35 states.

Revenue collections for fiscal 2015, which ended in most states on June 30, exceeded projections in nearly all of our larger states, including in our largest state Minnesota, where revenues for the fourth quarter alone are estimated to have exceeded the state’s February forecast by more than $450 million. Among our top 15 states, West Virginia is the only exception to this otherwise positive state revenue performance, as FY 2015 tax collections finished just short of budgeted estimates.

State budgets for fiscal year 2016, which began July 1, in most states, have been established in most states. And the news has been fairly positive with regard to funding for the services we provide across our 35 states, we remain in a very stable pricing environment. While it appears that the impact from across-the-board provider rate increases, we’ll be more modest in fiscal 2016 than it was in the last couple of fiscal years in several states, including Florida, New Jersey, and Ohio, provided funding in their new budgets to expand community-based services for individuals with intellectual and developmental disabilities.

This includes initiatives to transition individuals from public institutions into the community and also reduce the number of individuals who are on a waiting list for services. The biggest concern for us with regard to state policy is in the state of West Virginia. At the same time the state has to confront budget challenges, it is in the midst of renewing its I/DD waiver program, as it is required to do every five years.

We are keeping a close eye on the proposed plan, which still requires approval from CMS, as it would make significant changes to the waiver in an effort to control costs, families, providers, and advocates have all been outspoken in raising concerns. As we craft our plan for our fiscal 2016, we’re doing so with the goal of mitigating the impact of any changes in West Virginia.

Now, turning to our financial results, I want to briefly touch on the highlight for the quarter before turning the call over to Denis for more detail. Net revenue for the third quarter ended June 30, 2015, was $346 million, an increase of 8.7% from the year earlier period. Approximately 43% of net revenue growth was organic with acquisitions making up the balance. Adjusted EBITDA increased 13.8% to $39.1 million. These strong third quarter results reflect growth through selective acquisitions, as well as strong organic growth.

During the third quarter, we acquired five companies, bringing the total number of companies acquired during the first nine months of fiscal 2015 to 10. The five companies were acquired for a total consideration of $6.8 million. Four of those companies acquired in the third quarter are part of our human service segment, and provide a mix of residential and supported living services to individuals with intellectual and developmental disorders. The fifth acquisition adds to our Post-Acute Specialty Rehabilitation Services or SRS segment.

Moving forward, our overall M&A pipeline remains robust and I am pleased that this now includes a pipeline of Adult Day Health or ADH companies. Since entering this adjacent service last year via the acquisition of a platform company in Massachusetts, we have remained very enthusiastic about the potential of this new service line, as it is consistent with our mission of serving individuals in the least restrictive community settings and provides a strong value proposition for our payers and families.

Our ADH programs in Massachusetts, including the two new starts begun since the acquisition, have performed to our expectations and we are continuing to explore opportunities to expand our ADH footprint via organic and acquisitive growth. Our I/DD and SRS services, which generated approximately 65% and 20% respectively of our total net revenue in the third quarter of fiscal 2015, continue to perform very well with increases in both volume and average daily rates in the quarter compared to the same period the previous year.

For the third quarter 2015, I/DD gross revenue grew by 7.5% and SRS gross revenue grew by a strong 14.6%. As expected, our services for at-risk youth contracted. For the third quarter, ARY gross revenues were down $2.3 million or about 4.5% compared to the third quarter of 2014. As has been the case in the last couple of quarters, the decrease in revenues was primarily due to the reduction in our non-residential periodic services in North Carolina, resulting from statewide redesign of these programs.

In addition, our Illinois ARY programs declined approximately $1.5 million compared to Q3 of fiscal 2014. And as we announced during the third quarter, both North Carolina and Illinois are in the group of six states, where we are exiting ARY services, excluding the operations being discontinued in all six states, ARY gross revenues would have increased 3% in third quarter compared to the same quarter of 2014.

Before I summarize the year-to-date results, I want to provide a bit more context with regard to the future of our ARY services, following the recent completion of a strategic review of this service line. While we are reducing the size of our ARY footprint from 14 to 8 states, which we expect to contribute just north of 10% of our total revenue once each of these transitions is complete, we believe our actions including the establishment of a new Center of Excellence for Children and Family Services, composed of child welfare experts, will enhance our position as a national leader in serving youth-at-risk and their families.

We also believe that our actions will improve the financial performance and predictability of the ARY service line. Our decision to divest these operations is consistent with our strategic commitment to build leadership positions in our areas of strength. I am confident that these changes will make us better provider in a stronger company. Our ARY programs in Illinois ceased operation in May, and we continue to target our fiscal year-end on September 30 to complete the transition of ARY services in each of the other five states. So we may have to extend that timeframe to ensure the safety and well being of each young person transitioning to a new provider. This action will have no impact on our I/DD or SRS services provided in these states.

Looking at our year-to-date results, we’re pleased with the progress and growth we’ve achieved so far in fiscal 2015. During these nine months ended June 30, 2015, net revenues have increased 9.4%, including 15.3% for our SRS segment. We completed 10 acquisitions, with total annual revenues of approximately $38.5 million. We have taken action to strengthen and stabilize our ARY service line and we paid down debt and strengthened our financial position, which has resulted in enhanced free cash flow.

And finally, because our growth and financial performance is, of course, contingent on our service quality, clinical outcomes and client satisfaction; I want to take just a moment to highlight the results of a new study of our evidence-based ARY periodic Family Centered Treatment services or FCT in the State of Maryland.

Previous research has supported the effectives of FCT. And the results of this evaluation, which was independently led by the University of Maryland School of Social Work with funding support from our company, underscore the effectiveness and cost efficiency of the FCT versus those served in residential group homes. We expect that the university will soon release a full report.

At this point, I would like to turn the call over to Denis Holler, our Chief Financial Officer to discuss our financial results in more detail. Denis?

Denis Holler

Thanks, Bruce. I may refer to the quarter ended June 30, 2014 as the prior quarter and the quarter ended March 31, 2015 as last quarter. This quarter, the growth trends that have driven our business in the first two quarters of the year continued for the most part. Our I/DD and SRS sectors continued their grow trajectory both in total and organically.

Our ARY business has continued to lack, but the contraction in revenue is stabilizing compared to the prior quarter. New starts continue to drive growth in revenue and margin expansion, but to a lesser extent than prior quarters. There were no significant out of period adjustments that affected our results this quarter. Our expense related to our health care plans has increased to anticipated levels, but still contributed margin expansion this quarter.

Finally, working capital and accounts receivables collections remain positive, although DSO is about flat for the year compared with last year when we saw a significant improvement and the positive impact on free cash flow. As you will see, free cash flow for the quarter was robust, but I will be explaining a revision to the free cash flow number we reported in the second quarter.

Moving to the results, for this quarter net revenues grew by $27.8 million or 8.7% over the third quarter of last year. The increase included $12.1 million from organic growth and $15.7 million from acquisitions. Our Human Services segment, which contributed 80.5% of our total revenue, increased 7.4% over the prior quarter. Our SRS segment, which contributed 19.5% of third quarter revenues, increased 14.6% over the prior quarter.

Trends remained positive in volume and average rates for all of our service lines with the exception of the volumes in our at-risk youth business. This fueled our organic growth. This quarter I/DD gross revenues grew by 7.5% with more than half of that organic or 4.4%. SRS gross revenues grew at a rate of 14.6% with more than half of that organic or 7.9%. ARY revenues decreased by 4.5%, which was less than the prior quarter when they declined 9.5%, mainly as a result of the contraction of services in North Carolina.

Organic growth continued to be driven by the maturation of new starts. New starts started between fiscal 2012 and this quarter contributed $6.5 million of the growth in revenues or about half of our organic growth in revenue.

Investments in new starts declined from the prior quarter coming in into total spend of $1.4 million as compared to $1.8 million for the prior quarter. On a trailing 12-month basis, we invested $5.4 million compared to $6.1 million for the 12-month period ended prior quarter. As you know, this moderation was anticipated off of peak investment levels in 2012 and 2003, but is expected to continue in the current range going forward.

Adjusted EBITDA grew by 13.8% over the prior quarter with 4.1% coming from organic sources, in addition, our adjusted EBITDA margin improved by 50 basis points to 11.3%. Some of the important components of this net improvement are as follows.

It now appears that our high deductible consumer-driven healthcare plan design is gaining traction with our employee base, as more of our employees are utilizing the plan appropriately. We started to see this trend of increased claims in March and it is continued through the third quarter, although there is still a bit of uncertainty with this plan. We expect claims will level off but continue at the current higher level. This should stabilize our healthcare spend as a percent of revenue.

Despite the increasing claims activity, healthcare expense for the quarter was approximately $800,000 less than the prior quarter, which translates to a positive impact of 50 basis points on our EBITDA margin.

Continuing the trend from last quarter, we did not leverage direct labor. Excluding the impact of lower health expense discussed above, direct labor consumed an additional 40 basis points of margin.

The main cause of this increase was overtime, which is typically an indicator of increased vacant positions and turnover. We have started to see increased competition for labor in some markets, and as a result, last year, instituted our variable pay plan for direct care employees.

As we mentioned last quarter, we are contemplating a potential expansion of that program, should we have the capacity to do so. Continuing the trend from recent quarters, occupancy costs increased absorbing 50 basis points of margin. Although this has happened for a number of reasons in the past, this quarter it appeared to be isolated to lower occupancy in our I/DD service line, in both our waiver group homes and intermediate care facilities. This reduction in occupancy was not isolated, but appears to be temporary.

Continuing the trend from the last two quarters, we continue to experience a reduction in sales adjustments to our revenue. Net deductions from our revenue declined by approximately $440,000 over the prior quarter and contributed about 25 basis points to our margin. Finally, we were able to continue our trend of leveraging general and administrative expenses, including indirect labor, in the quarter which accounted for 30 basis points of margin expansion.

Net income for the quarter was $0.6 million compared to $5,000 for the prior quarter. This increase or the increase in net income was primarily due to lower interest expense resulting from the refinancing of our senior credit facility in January of 2014 and the redemption of our senior notes in October of 2014 using the net proceeds from the initial public offering. This reduction was more than offset by the increase in stock-based compensation expense of $1.3 million and $8.2 million impairment charge, resulting from the planned disposition of our at-risk youth businesses in North Carolina, Texas, Florida, Indiana and Louisiana. The increase in our stock-based compensation expense came from an employee incentive plan established in connection with our IPO.

Earnings per share was 1% for the third quarter ended June 30, 2015, compared to zero for the prior quarter. However, excluding the impairment charge related to the ARY closure states indicated earlier, earnings per share would have been $0.14. This quarter we are revising the free cash flow reported in Q2 from $16.3 million to $10.2 million. And the year-to-date free cash flow reported in the second quarter from $1.7 million to a negative a $4.4 million. The $6.1 million difference was due to the erroneous inclusion of the contingent consideration for the Castle acquisition and the change in liabilities on the statements of cash flow.

This quarter, free cash flows remains strong, coming in at $24.7 million and $20.4 million on a year-to-date basis, these numbers compared to $32.4 million $42.4 million in the prior year. The reduction in free cash flow was caused for the most part by the significant improvement in days sales outstanding on accounts receivable in the prior year, which was approximately 3.5 days, compared to the relatively flat performance this year at around 42 days. We were 42.8 days at quarter end.

Also significant were the IPO and debt refinancing costs which were incurred this year, but did not impact the first three quarters of last year. Capital expenditures for the quarter were $10.6 million or 3.1% of net revenue, which is in line with our expectations and consistent with both last quarter and the prior quarter. Typically, we experienced a higher CapEx spend in our fourth quarter, however, which is why we have set our guidelines at 3% to 3.5%. Year-to-date capital expenditures are running at 3% of net revenue.

Finally, this is the first quarter where our net leverage dipped below four times as we came in at 3.91 times, our trailing 12 month adjusted EBITDA of $150.2 million.

Now, turning to guidance for our fiscal year ended September 30, 2015. We are narrowing our net revenue guidance range, which was $1.345 billion to $1.385 billion to between $1.355 billion and $1.375 billion. We are confirming our existing guidance range for adjusted at between $152 million and $154 million. Last quarter, we announced that in response to wage pressure in some states, we were considering an expansion of our variable pay program for direct service employees in the event that we exceeded the top-end of the EBITDA range.

This is still our view and as a result, the top-end of our adjusted EBITDA range of $154 million will likely be a ceiling on our results for the fiscal year. The revenue and adjusted EBITDA guidance includes the operations of the ARY businesses slated for closure or divestiture, but does not include any one-time expenses, which will be treated as an add-back to adjusted EBITDA.

And with that, I will turn it back to Bruce.

Thank you, Denis. In summary, we are pleased with the performance of our business as we enter the fourth quarter of fiscal 2015. Our industry dynamics are attractive. We are executing on our proven business model, and we’re excited about the opportunities that lie ahead. I’m very grateful to all of our outstanding workforce and thank them for their continued hard work and dedication, and I would like to thank all of our shareholders. I look forward to keeping you updated on the Civitas story as we move forward. So thank you all.
