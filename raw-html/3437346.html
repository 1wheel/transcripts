Pfenex's (PFNX) CEO Bertrand Liang on Q2 2015 Results - Earnings Call Transcript

Aug. 13, 2015 1:46 PM ET  |  About: Pfenex (PFNX) by: SA Transcripts

Pfenex (NYSEMKT:PFNX)

Q2 2015 Earnings Conference Call

August 13, 2015 08:30 AM ET

Executives

Paul Wagner - CFO

Bertrand Liang - CEO

Analysts

Jason Butler - JMP Securities

Douglas Tsao - Barclays

Peter Lawson - Mizuho Securities

Andy Shay - William Blair

Operator

Greetings. Ladies and gentlemen, and welcome to the Pfenex Second Quarter 2015 Results and Business Update Call. A brief question-and-answer session will follow the formal presentation. As a reminder, this conference is being recorded. [Operator Instructions] It is now my pleasure to introduce your host, Dr. Paul Wagner, Pfenex’s Chief Financial Officer. Paul, you may now begin.

Paul Wagner

Thank you Andrew, and good morning. Welcome to Pfenex’s Second quarter 2015 conference call. Earlier today we release the financial results for the second quarter ended June 30, 2015 and the earnings release and the corporate presentation are available on the Investor Relation section of our website. On the call today are Dr. Bert Liang, our Chief Executive Officer, and myself. Bert will provide a summary of key events as well as provide a business update, and then I’ll describe the second quarter financial results. Bert will then open the call up for Q&A.

Bear with me for just a minute here while read the Safe Harbor statements, I like to remind you that some of the statements made during the call today are forward-looking statements, including statements regarding our expectations for the developments plans for our product candidates, the expected timeline and release of results from our clinical trials, the initiation of additional clinical trials and our future projections related to increases and expenses and reductions in protein production revenue.

Actual results could differ materially from those contemplated by our forward-looking statements and reported results should not be considered as indicative of future performance. Additional information about the risks and other factors that could potentially impact our financial result is included in today's press release and then our filings with the SEC including our Form 10-K and the most recent Form 10-Q we filed with the SEC. The forward-looking statements in this call are based on information available to us as of today’s date, and we disclaim any obligation to update any forward-looking statements except as required by law.

Bertrand Liang

Great. Thank you, Paul. Good morning, and welcome to the Pfenex quarterly conference call. It's actually been quite busy in the Biosimilar industry this past several months. We are pleased to see the ongoing clarity from court rulings regarding the development and regulatory pathway for biosimilars moving forward. We continue being encouraged on the ongoing efforts in the courts certain facets the pathway clarifying areas of the Biologics Price Competition Innovation Act or BCIA as our programs move forward.

Indeed we have continued to make progress in the second quarter inventing our portfolio product candidates. In support of the advancement of our pipeline and operations in April we successfully completed a public follow on offering of common stock for both primary and secondary shares of certain existing shareholders were offered. The company proceeds were approximately $37 million net of the underwriting discounts commissions and estimated operating expenses.

As a business update the collaboration with Hospira on our PF582 molecule our biosimilar candidate Lucentis is going well. We continue to assist in the manufacturing technology transfer of PF582 in preparation for the comparative clinical study expected to begin in 2016.

Pfenex also initiated a Phase I trial of PF530 a biosimilar candidate of Betaseron in March. The trials are single dose crossover study and has enrolled 12 subjects randomized to receive PF530 in Betaseron. This is a pharmacokinetic study in which we also collecting pharmacodynamic and biomarker endpoints. We continue to expect to have a readout from this study in the second half of 2015.

We continue to expect PF708, our peptide generic to Forteo, to enter a bioequivalent study in the second half of 2015 and expect to initiate the Phase 1 trial for our recombinant anthrax vaccine in the second half of 2015 as well. As you know this latter program is entirely funded through the U.S. government.

Now, I’d like to turn the call back over to Paul to review the second quarter 2015 financial results.

Paul Wagner

Thank you, Bert. Now turning to the financial results for the second quarter of 2015. Total revenue is 2.3 million in the two months period ended June 30 and that compared to 3.3 million in the same period in 2014. The decrease in revenue was due to the stage of development of our anthrax vaccine at the Px563L under the government contract and a decrease in activity related to the protein production services work. Was partially offset by an increase in the license revenue and sales of our reagent protein products.

We do expect revenue related to the protein production services to decline in the near term as we are shifting those researchers to continue to develop our own internal pipeline. On the cost of revenue that decreased 0.9 million in the three months period of June 30, 2015 down from 2.5 million in the same period in 2014. The decrease in that cost of revenue is due primarily to the stage of development of the Px563L product candidate under our government contracts so we do have a pass through and those pass-through are recognized in that cost of revenue. Given the nature of the novel vaccine development process those cost are going to be fluctuating and again does depend on the stage of development.

Research and development expenses increased to 3.6 million in the three months period ended June 30, that’s up from 0.9 million in the same period in 2014. And that increase in research and development expenses was due to the increased development activity of our product candidates primarily PF708 that’s generic to Forteo and PF530 to biosimilar to Betaseron. We also did hire some additional personnel in our research and development.

PF530 recall was removed from the joint development license agreement with Strides back in February 2015 and we did initiate as Bert mention the Phase 1 trial for PF530 in March of 2015. We do expect the research and development costs are going to be increasing as we go forward as we independently advanced PF530 as a wholly owned product candidate. In addition we do expect R&D expenses to increase in the third and fourth quarter of 2015 as we advance our other lead candidates and pipeline product candidates.

Our SG&A expense did increase to 3.7 million in the three months period ended June 30 compared to 2 million in the same period a year ago. The increases in SG&A expenses were due to an increase in activities associated with operating as a publicly traded company. We do expect G&A cost to increase the productivity associated with operating as a public including maintaining compliance requirements and potentially additional hiring of personnel.

Net loss for the second quarter was 6 million that compared to 2.6 million in the second quarter of 2014 and the loss per share was at $0.27 compared to a loss of $1.67 in the year ago period.

Weighted average shares outstanding was 22.5 in the cash and the cash equivalents as of June 30, was 123 million, that includes the 37 million that we received in the proceeds from the follow-on offering in April.

Bertrand Liang

Great. Thank you, Paul. That actually concludes our prepared comments. I’ll turn it over to Andrew the operator to open the call for questions if there are any.

Question-and-Answer Session

Operator

We will now begin the question-and-answer session. [Operator Instructions] The first question comes from Jason Butler of JMP Securities. Please go ahead.

Jason Butler

I guess Bert can I just ask you to expand on your financial comments about the court rulings on the biosimilar space, I am pointing specifically where you think we really got clarity? And then I guess after that what you're looking for still to get clarity from as you look to becoming a commercial biosimilar company over the next couple of years?

Bertrand Liang

I think that interesting aspects around the Amgen Lucentis case was that there was a significant amount of clarity with regards to the optionality of what’s called the patent dance, you're basically the patent speed mechanism. As you know that went through district and the federal circuit courts for determination, whether or not that component of the law was actually required by case sponsors. Now we’ve had a fairly definitive notation that this is an option that allows for case sponsors to either go down the patent dance or it allows us to actually avoid that and enter into litigation in a different way. So I think that that level of clarity is something that needed to be certainly put forth by the courts so that we as case sponsors will have more clarity around what our options are going forward.

In addition, the 180 day notification presuming that a company does not go down, the patent dance part of the law 351L that actually is also a good thing because it just shows what needs to happen and what are the ramifications of moving that -- moving towards the pathway of not going down the patent dance.

This as you also know there is another case out there currently and that involves Amgen and [indiscernible] where the patent dance actually been consummated, where they have actually done the patent dance and now we’ll see some additional clarity there whether or not the 180 day notification period is going to be required at the time of approval or is it going to can actually happen earlier. So all of these cases are good because what they allow us to do is not only understand what the law requires, but also shows us what our optionality is going forward with regards to being a case sponsor and moving our products from development into commercialization.

Paul Wagner

And I just want to add to that, I mean I think that the appeals court ruling does provide an extra degree of freedom with respect to the patent dance in exchange of the patents and I think if just thinking through this if there are no patents that are in existence as a potential to opt out of the patent dance and potentially be able to access the market on a more accelerated basis. But so, when would a case sponsor potentially want to enter into the patent dance, I think if you’ve got either weak or strong patents you may want to enter into that patent dance because that’s final list as you go through that dance you end up with a final list.

Now if you believe that those patents from the reference company are particularly strong that finalist will go through accelerated litigation and so you’ll be able to map out what the landscape of the IP looks like and likewise if there is weak IP you're not going to get a preliminary injunction on that final list reference company, you're going to get a preliminary injunction on that final list. And so do you have the potential to be able to launch at risk if you believe those patents are weak. But again I think that the appeals court ruling just provides case sponsors with an extra degree of freedom based on what they believe is the landscape of IP.

Jason Butler

And then just a last question on PF530, can you talk about or is it primarily this is a safety and PK study, can you talk about what the parameters for success in terms of PK are here in this Phase 1 study? And then what are the measures of -- I want to say [askew] of biologic activity that you’ll be looking for we might see in the top-line for lease?

Bertrand Liang

Sure so for PF activity that’s our interferon data, [indiscernible] for that obviously what we’re looking for is a similarity in pharmacokinetics, but we’re also looking at pharmacodynamics parameters as well and what we’re going to be doing with that particular data is really reflective of what the whitepaper from the EMA has suggested with regards to correlating PK and PD. So with regards to success it's basically going to be - to be able to recapitulate some of the suggestions from that particular white paper from the EU.

Operator

The next question comes from Douglas Tsao of Barclays. Please go ahead.

Douglas Tsao

So first of all maybe if you could provide if any sort of update on the 529 program?

Bertrand Liang

Sure, so the 529 program is our [last] program, that’s actually earlier stage program with respect to our biosimilar programs currently. We're in the midst of doing a significant amount of process development within that particular program. And we expect to be able to give you some additional updates going forward in the second half of this year with respect to that but it's moving along currently and the team that's working on that here is really trying to push that thing ahead expeditiously.

Jason Butler

And then second question would be just if there is activity ongoing in the Strides partnership right now? And how should we think about that from a product development standpoint on a go forward basis?

Bertrand Liang

Sure so step that program is continuing to move forward. We are working with our partnership at Stride in a couple of meetings that we had over the past maybe three or so months. We've been discussing the different products that are in that partnership and I think it continues to be something that we want to do that takes advantage of not only our capabilities with cutting edge technology, but their capabilities as well with regards to their understanding of a variety of different emerging as well as global market issues. So that continues to move forward, it's not something that has found by the wayside, we're strategizing on an active basis with respect to our colleagues over there in Bangalore.

Operator

The next question comes from Peter Lawson of Mizuho Securities. Please go ahead.

Peter Lawson

Bert, I wonder if you could just talk about the manufacturing transfer PF582 to Hospira. How's that’s going and when do you think that transfer will be complete?

Bertrand Liang

For manufacturing the tech transfer to PF582 our partners at Hospira has been a little bit earlier is going really well. And we've had active engagement with the Hospira team. And we definitely anticipate that will be completed by the end of the year. And to maintain our timelines with regards to charting a comparative funds next year. The team from Hospira and the manufacturing team from Pfenex have been working literally on a week by week basis to ensure that all of the -- not only the tech transfer but as you know the subtleties around the different types of a detail associated with I think from fermentation all the way through to equipment is replicated appropriately over at Hospira for their manufacturing. So that’s really going well and I have to say the teams are working really hard in order to get that accomplished on time.

Paul Wagner

And we do have regular meetings of the executives hearing committee and I'll say that our most recent meeting has been very, very collaborative we've had very good interactions with the team at Hospira and it has been communicated to us as well at Pfizer is also supportive of the program.

Peter Lawson

Will there be any personnel that goes with that tech transfer or how does that work just the kind of the internal know how transfer.

Bertrand Liang

Obviously we continue to be part of the manufacturing team as the collaboration was forward. This could not transfer personnel however, it's basically just the manufacturing teams from both sides are going to be actively speaking with regards to making the products. So while there is a true active collaborations that cannot transfer our people per se.

Peter Lawson

Then on 530 just back to the kind of the dates released in the second half. What should we expect to see? Was it just PK and PD data or is that early efficacy data as well?

Bertrand Liang

The study is actually being done in subjects of that they are novel volunteers. So there is not efficacy per se so it is a PD kind of evaluation and no more volunteers.

Peter Lawson

And then Paul just a minor housekeeping. How quickly should think about the protein production service business declining and any cost associated with that?

Paul Wagner

Yes, so again there is a small amount of the protein production services that remains that’s related in part to some vaccine work that we're doing for path. So again we've been communicating pretty regularly that we want to be using that internal capacity for own product development. I think that what's where we're creating the most shareholder value and that's where our focus is. And we have seen that decline I think we're going to see sort of a flattening of the declines, I think what we've seen in this quarter is relatively representative.

Operator

The next question comes from Andy Shay of William Blair. Please go ahead.

Andy Shay

I have a question about the generic Forteo PF708. We're just wondering if you can provide more color on how the company can the leverage the platform from a cost efficiency perspective given the fact that right now Forteo has been producing [indiscernible]. Thanks.

Bertrand Liang

So the Pfenex expression technology is a very efficient way of making Forteo it's really a cutting edge manufacturing and strain engineering engine. And as a result we’ve been able to be very successful in creating a strain that is incredibly efficient to produce the [indiscernible] that makes up Forteo. So as a result of that our ability to be very competitive with regards to or our overall cost of goods is significant.

So I think that what that I -- I think we should this in the past, the level of productivity of our particular strain for this molecule is we can pretty much make the world supply of this particular peptide in very, very conservative volumes. So I think that to a large extent our advantage is the expression technology has allowed us not only to do this incredibly efficiently, but also cost effective and really with a significant amount of avoidance of real and opportunity of costs, particularly given the experience that we’ve had in the past when we were a service provider for a large cap biopharma.

So that’s one of the advantages of having worked with other companies in the past making difficult to express proteins. You really learn how to hone the edge of your technology so that you can be as efficient as possible.

Operator

This concludes our question-and-answer session. I would like to turn the conference back over to Dr. Bert Liang, CEO for any closing remarks.

Bertrand Liang

Again we would like to thank everybody for their participation and their presence on the call today. So again thanks very much from the company. Thank you.

Operator

The conference has now concluded. Thank you for attending today’s presentation. You may now disconnect.
