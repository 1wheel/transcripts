Peter Liguori - President, Chief Executive Officer & Director

But before I more deeply dive into our results for Q2, I do want to take a moment to talk about all the recent noise in the media environment because we are growing and we're thriving in spite of all of it. We're confident that our commitment to providing great value to our audiences, advertisers and affiliates, gives us a competitive advantage and insulates us from many of the issues impacting our competitors. Moreover, our long-term strategies will enable us to sustain profitable growth, notwithstanding the rapid changes taking place in the media landscape.

In our Broadcast business, for example, we're growing revenue and building market share in a flattish core advertising market by emphasizing local news, live sports and a deep connection to the communities we serve. We're growing net retrans dollars at a rate of 20% year-over-year. We're growing our Digital revenue at a strong double-digit pace. We're growing our diginet revenue at Antenna TV and THIS TV. As the whole debate over the so-called skinny bundles and who's in, who's out, we believe in the value of great content. For broadcasters, that means leading local news and live sports. The new entrants in the distribution game, the Sonys and Apples of this world, have confirmed our belief, declaring publicly that they want local content, our kind of content. That makes us essential in any size bundle. At our cable network, WGN America, despite bundles and cord shaving, we're growing distribution, not losing it. We expect revenues at WGNA to grow 20% annually, and that we'll see profits in 2016. With the explosion of distribution channels, content offerings and devices, navigational solutions that help guide consumer choice are becoming increasingly critical. Core advertising, excluding political, up; digital advertising is up significantly. Retrans and carriage fees, up; in fact, in every significant driver of revenue, in the largest segments of our Television business were up. This resulted in higher production costs in this quarter. But let me be crystal clear; our investment in these shows have not changed. Same shows, same license fee, same investment in ownership of the shows. It's just that our expenses are hitting disproportionately in this quarter. And we won't see the benefits of our investment in the shows, whether from SVOD deals, domestic or international or international distribution and other secondary revenue streams, until next year and beyond.

In the case of Manhattan, just as one example, we already have a Hulu deal in place. In addition, our partner, Lionsgate, has sold the international rights into 17 international markets and growing. But we're not in a position yet to book that revenue. The cash will materialize later when we and our partners begin airing the show.

In addition, second quarter adjusted EBITDA and operating profits were impacted by a change in the timing of amortization for Salem and Manhattan, which had the effect of shifting roughly $14 million of amortization expense to 2015 from 2016. The lion's share of that $14 million change, about $13 million of it, also burdened our books in Q2. Chandler's going to walk you through a more detailed look at our overall financials in a moment, and he'll dig deeper into those at WGNA.

Now, those of you who I've met in person and over the phone know that I always state that I prefer to talk performance over promise because it's performance that ultimately counts. As such, I'm going to ask you to please bear with me as I detail our measurable positive matrix that illustrate the strength of our current operations in the current environment.

So, let's start by taking a deeper diver into the largest segment of our business, TV and Entertainment, where, I said earlier, the advertising environment is kind of flattish. The TV and Entertainment quarterly revenues were up 4% to more than $445 million. Core advertising revenue was up a little more than 2%. We achieved revenue growth, even as total advertising spending declined a bit in many of our markets. We grew core advertising market share in all of our top seven markets, and we grew share across all 42 stations by about half a point.

Results at our biggest station were particularly strong in Q2. Core advertising at PIX here in New York was up more than 20%. Share was up two points. At KTLA, ad revenue was up more than 8% and share was up more than a half point. And in Chicago, core ad revenue was up 1% and market share grew more than a full point.

Digital, very important on our front. Digital advertising revenue across our stations grew 24% in the quarter. Retransmission revenues were up 23%. And we're maintaining our subscriber base and we're also actually increasing our subscriber base.

These results are a clear demonstration that our programming strategies are working on our station front. When you look at news, our commitment to being the local station of record continues to draw audiences and drive rating growth. Many of our markets have very good May sweeps period. Let me quickly run through a few quick highlights. PIX saw total news ratings across all time periods increase 14% over the last year. KTLA, which is number one in morning news, saw a healthy 12% increase in the critical 10 PM news. In Chicago, WGN's 4 PM news was up 50%, and its 5 PM news was up 31%. And finally, at our CW station in Denver, morning news was up as much as 60% depending on the time period.

Our commitment to live local sports is also working, especially with the Yankees, Mets, and Cubs in the thick of the race. In New York, my first-place Mets, with their ratings on PIX are up 30% over the games we carried last second quarter. On nights when we had baseball leading into news at PIX, albeit the Mets or the Yankees, ratings for news were up more than 56%, showing the power of the great local sport lead-ins into our local news. In Chicago, same story, season-to-date ratings for the Cubs are up 95% versus a year ago. This strength in local content, along with our new common website platform, is driving both traffic and digital revenue.

Unique visitors to our websites in Q2 reached more than 80 million, that's about up 20% compared to last year. And we also saw sequential growth in traffic in the first half of the year as well. That drove Digital revenues higher by 24%.

Finally, the strength of our TV stations and their news operations and related websites put us in a great position for next year's presidential election. Again, we have 13 stations in battleground states, many with contested primaries to the Senate as well as the Presidency.

Now, let's turn to WGN America. Again, this is a network growing audience, growing subscribers and growing carriage fees by building a recognizable brand. In fact, WGNA is increasing audience, even if the broader ad-supported cable industry is talking about losing them. Specifically, if you look at total day, the audiences of mature cable networks in the first quarter and second quarter declined 10%, 8%, respectively. During that same time on a total-day basis, our audience of WGN America increased 7% in Q1 and 11% in Q2.

The difference in primetime between WGNA and our competitors is even more dramatic. While the primetime audience for mature cable networks was down 9%, WGN audience was up 8% in the quarter. And remember, these audience gains are being generated without the benefit of Season 2 of Manhattan or our exclusive syndicated programs like Persons of Interest and Elementary, all of which are going to join WGNA's lineup in the fourth quarter. These kinds of audience gains are helping us write good business in the upfront. We're seeing double-digit increase in CPM rates for our original and exclusive syndicated series. Quality programming and larger audiences are driving value for our cable and satellite partners.

These things are making WGNA more attractive to them, and they're accelerating WGNA's conversion to a basic cable network. As such, WGNA's carriage revenues were up 51%, driven by higher per-sub fees, and more encouragingly, an actual increase of subscribers of about 2% this year.

On the programming front, Season 2 of Salem delivered on all of our expectations and furthered our strategic branding objectives for the network. It has a large loyal audience of more than 1 million viewers a week, which is hugely important for a growing network like WGNA. We're also excited about Season 2 of Manhattan, which launches October 13 and which some critics have called a dramatic gem.

Looking ahead to the fourth quarter, we should be very strong, because in addition to Manhattan, WGNA will add two new extremely popular exclusive syndicated programs to our lineup, namely Elementary and Persons of Interest. We expect WGNA ratings to jump again for two reasons. First, these programs are even more popular in the first run than Blue Bloods, which we launched earlier this year and has increased audience by as much as 80% wherever we've run it. Second, in contrast to and in improving upon Blue Bloods, we have the exclusive cable rights to both Elementary and PoI. In fact, two-thirds of WGNA's primetime programming will be all new in Q4. These gains were fueled in large part by last year's acquisition of HWW, Baseline and What's ON India. The big news in the second quarter was the strategic acquisition of three sports data companies: namely, Infostrada, SportsDirect, Covers, and the launch of Gracenote Sports.

These acquisitions made Gracenote the first global provider of data for music, video and sports, and they enable the company to provide one-stop solutions for a roster of blue-chip clients in the U.S. and around the world, clients such as Toyota, Ford, Roku, Google and Apple.

Like music and video data, demand for sports entertainment is exploding. Infostrada Sports and SportsDirect produce and distribute the most extensive set of sports data in North America, Europe and Asia, with critical information in data for all the major professional sports leagues around the world, including the NFL, NBA, NHL, Major League Baseball, European Football and the Olympics.

Gracenote will tap into these data sets and leverage its own entertainment data experience to create new data-driven sports products and services for fantasy sports and fan applications, as well as a variety of online broadcast and print media.

And these acquisitions are already gaining traction. For example, Gracenote just won its first sports deal from The Canadian Broadcasting Company to bring regional sports scores and its statistics to CBC's online properties.

And Gracenote won the deal over several incumbents. Gracenote is also deepening its relationship with Apple and Google. Last year, Apple chose to power its international streaming music service. In the second quarter of this year, as they prepare to launch Apple Music here in the States, Apple again came to Gracenote.

Today, our data helps to power the playlists in radio stations of Apple Music for millions of fans, something we're incredibly proud of. Gracenote also expanded its business relationship with Google to provide geo-listings for the search giant in 10 different regions across the globe.

On the automotive side, almost every major automaker is now embracing Gracenote technology in one form or another, with a couple of significant agreements being renewed or expanded in Q2. For example, we renewed our long-standing agreement to provide music technology and data in Toyota vehicles for several more years to come.

And we expanded our long-term relationship with BMW, making the company's new 7 Series vehicle, the first to carry Gracenote's newest innovation called MusicID radio. MusicID radio brings together traditional radio with popular streaming services like Napster and Rhapsody. In sum, Gracenote is a business on the rise, the leader in the space, well positioned to take advantage of the explosion in data and digital devices and grow its revenues and margins.

Before I close, I want to talk a little bit about another one of our valuable assets, namely spectrum. The FCC is targeting an auction at the end of the first quarter of 2016, and we're ramping up our preparations accordingly. We've been active with the FCC together with Fox, Univision, and Ion, working with FCC staff on the rule-making process. And we're leaning forward on potential approaches to the auction, including channel sharing in major markets, which may allow us to monetize spectrum with minimal impact to our operating revenues and profits. As I've said before, we have spectrum in very valuable markets, and we're keenly focused on the potential opportunities presented by the auction.

To wrap up, our operations is thriving, and in the face of secular challenges, we're growing audience, subscribers and advertiser and affiliate revenues. Our Digital and Data business is winning new clients and deepening its business with its existing blue-chip client roster. We're mindfully choosing to invest in our businesses. We can slow down or speed up these investments as we see fit. We are simply not sitting still. We're generating strong revenue growth. We help produce significant cash flow, augmented by what has historically been $200 million in annual dividends from our equity stakes (23:06) in Food Network and CareerBuilder. We're excited by what lies ahead and the value this company is bringing and will continue to bring to shareholders for years to come.

Excluding political and the revenue generated by airing the Super Bowl on 14 FOX stations last year and only two NBC stations this year, our consolidated operating revenue grew 8% in the first half of 2015. Consolidated adjusted EBITDA decreased $47 million or 18% to $221 million in the first half of 2015. In addition, we have already received $129 million of cash from our equity investments in the first half of this year.

As Peter just described, this growth is driven by market share gains across our largest markets. Adjusted EBITDA declined in the second quarter 2015, primarily due to increases in programming expenses and increases in compensation as we continue to expand our profitable news operations and invest in our sales operations.

As we mentioned, the primary driver of the increase in our expenses is due to increases in programming costs. First, we experienced higher amortization of license fees for Salem and Manhattan due to the change in the timing of amortization of our first-run original programs as we've discussed with you last quarter. As Peter mentioned, in the second quarter, we incurred $13 million of the $14 million of increased amortization that we disclosed we would incur this year.

Second, we incurred product costs at Tribune Studios for the second season of Manhattan as well as the first seasons of our new co-owned original programs, Underground and Outsiders, both of which went into production during the second quarter, and are anticipated to air in the first half of next year. Note that we do not anticipate that these acquisitions will have a material impact on our results in the remainder of 2015. In terms of the remainder of the year, we are reaffirming our full year guidance for Digital and Data and expect that as the past year, the segment will have a stronger relative fourth quarter.

Now turning to Corporate and Other, which includes the results of our real estate operations. In the second quarter of 2015, real estate revenue was $12 million. This was offset by real estate expenses and corporate expenses, resulting in a negative impact to adjusted EBITDA of $19 million. Increases in corporate expenses were mainly a result of the implementation of new technology and back-office infrastructure. These new technologies and services are primarily being implemented and certain functions and systems were separated as part of the spin-off of Tribune Publishing last year.

Now from a balance sheet perspective, we ended the quarter with cash at $404 million. In the first half of the year, our distributions to shareholders and including dividends and stock repurchases totaled $854 million. In June of this year, we successfully refinanced our term loan facility with proceeds from the issuance of a $1.1 billion 5.875% senior note. This activity resulted in a charge to our P&L of $37 million for a loss on extinguishment of debt. This refinancing has resulted in a revision of our cash interest downward from $140 million to approximately $130 million as our first semiannual interest payment on the new notes will occur at the beginning of next year. Our cash tax guidance number was also impacted by the refinancing, among other things, and is now expected to be between $95 million and $100 million in 2015.

On August 1, 2015, we entered into an agreement to sell all of our interest in Newsday Holdings, LLC, which will result in a payment of $103 million of the existing deferred tax liability that is recorded on our balance sheet. This tax payment is expected to be paid later this year. Our previously authorized stock repurchase program remains in place. As of the end of the second quarter, we had repurchased approximately $253 million of the $400 million authorized under the program. And as of yesterday, we had repurchased $333 million of the $400 million authorized, leaving $67 million available for repurchases under the current program.

That concludes my remarks, and we'll now open up the lines for questions. And thank you very much.
