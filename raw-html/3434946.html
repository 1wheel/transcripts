E.ON's (ENAKF) CEO Johannes Teyssen on Q2 2015 Results - Earnings Call Transcript

E.ON SE (OTCPK:ENAKF) Q2 2015 Earnings Conference Call August 12, 2015 5:00 AM ET

Anke Groth

Thank you very much. Dear analysts and investors, a warm welcome to E.ON’s Half Year Results 2015. Our CEO, Johannes Teyssen, and our CFO, Michael Sen are here together with me.

We will start with Johannes Teyssen, who will give you an update regarding the progress of our spinoff process, as well as our major new-build projects before speaking about the regulatory and political developments in the last couple of months. Afterwards, Michael Sen will guide you through our half year results. The remaining time is reserved for your questions.

Johannes, over to you.

Johannes Teyssen

Thank you, Anke. Good morning, ladies and gentlemen, also from me. Welcome to our first half 2015 conference call.

Before I start my presentation, I’d like to introduce and warmly welcome our new CFO, Michael Sen. Michael joined us in June from Siemens. He worked there for almost 30 years and comes with an impressive track record. He held a broad variety of management roles in both operations and strategy. These include being responsible for international portfolio strategies at Siemens corporate development and which is important for he as serving as Head of Investor Relations. In his most recent role, he worked for more than six years as CFO of Siemens Healthcare, a business which roughly employs 43,000 people worldwide. Within this business, Michael was the driving force for significant efficiency enhancements and portfolio streamlining. He earned himself rightfully the reputation of putting a very strong focus on capital efficiency.

Furthermore, he gained firsthand experience at Siemens in carving out major businesses and preparing them for public listing. I’m thus delighted to have him on board. Within E.ON, we are happy that we can profit from his broader experiences. I look very much forward to working together with him and making the implementation of our strategy a big success for all stakeholders.

In addition, I’d like to take the opportunity to congratulate our former CFO, Klaus Schäfer for the designation to become the CEO of Uniper. I thank Klaus for his outstanding job as CFO. Furthermore, I’m convinced that with his strong background, particularly in Uniper’s core businesses, he is best suited for this position.

Finally, dear analysts and investors, I’d like to thank you very much for the high participation rate in our recent investor study. Your feedback was very much appreciated, and it will feed into our further work as a really helpful input for internal work to set up the two new companies.

With this, I’m moving to chart one. Let’s now move on to the results of the first half of 2015. As expected, EBITDA was down roughly 13% and reached €4.3 billion. The decline is largely driven by lower achieved prices in power and oil, the weak ruble, as well disposal effects. The positive effects we envisage from the finalization of several large projects are all geared towards the second half of this year and will become thus more prominent the following quarters. Underlying net income came out at €1.2 billion.

With regards to the full year outlook, we are well underway to reach our earnings targets. Thus, we keep the full year 2015 outlook unchanged. We continue to expect EBITDA to come out between €7 billion and €7.6 billion and underlying net income between €1.4 billion and €1.8 billion. Furthermore, we confirm our commitment to pay a dividend of €0.50 a share for the year 2015. Michael will detail the results in a moment. Before he does so, I would like to first update you on the spinoff preparation; secondly highlight the progress of our major projects in renewables, Russia and conventional generation; and finally discuss recent regulatory and political developments.

Chart two: Ever since we announced our decision to separate our group into two distinctively focused companies, we have heard from quite a number of investors that they think our spinoff execution process just takes too long. To provide a better understanding for the necessary length of the process time line, I would like to give you a look into the engine room of the project which we call One2Two.

For this measure, I reemphasize that the whole split process is not about spinning off a relatively small and already existing subsidiary which leaves the rest of the group largely unaffected, it is rather about creating two completely new companies, which implies essentially a full restructuring, in large part the whole group. Technically, we perform this task by creating Uniper. Its respective assets and activities will be cast out from a variety of E.ON’s current setups. This involves a very substantial amount of legal and organizational restructuring as well as a lot of redesigning of operational processes and sometimes even relocations. The carve-out work stream of the One2Two project identified in the diagnostic phase until April; more than 4,000 individual aspects that have to be taken care of in this carve-out phase. According to our external advisors, this is still in the normal range but typical for such a complex operation.

The team from the carve-out work stream translated the listed issues into a very concrete and specific implementation plan. Today, the roll-out of this plan to operationally separate the two companies is in full swing and roughly one-third of the more than 400,000 milestones are already achieved. To make the already achieved progress more tangible for you, I’d like to highlight some of the key achievements so far.

We allocated all of E.ON’s roughly 1,300 legal entities to either Uniper or future E.ON. Furthermore, we finalized the new organizational structures for both companies and agreed this co-determination. These structures were the necessary preconditions to appoint besides the new two top management teams, the roughly 350 executives for future E.ON and Uniper, and all have been named by now to new roles. And we expect all our employees to be allocated to the two companies by the mid of November.

As E.ON Global Commodities shall be part of Uniper, the setting up of the new function, E.ON, energy management, has started. The new function shall procure energy and market future E.ON’s renewables output. It shall cooperate very closely with the local sales teams. To provide procurement and marketing services and capabilities locally, new teams shall be established in Germany, Sweden and the UK. They shall be managed centrally across the regions from Essen. Their main task shall be to provide market access, spot optimization, as well as dispatch. It will however not be a new trading house; it will be clearly small and functional.

Let us move on to the overall project timeline and the key milestones, a chart which all of you have become quite familiar with, on chart three. Drawing conclusions from my words on our achieved progress, I can reassure you today that our internal preparation for one of the world’s largest spinoffs in the energy business is well on track, and that future E.ON and Uniper shall be operationally separated from January 1, 2016 onwards. In 2016, the focus of our project will then shift from a carve-out phase towards a spinoff, in a transaction sense. Of course, also the transaction work stream within One2Two is in full swing already and we have, for example started to draft a prospectus for Uniper’s listing. From the day of the announcement of the spinoff back in December last year, we made very clear that we intend to fully divest our remaining minority share in Uniper step wise over the medium-term. However, this divestment process shall not start before the beginning of 2018. This is due to a number of reasons. Besides capital market considerations, we are also to take into account some tax related measures.

By initially spinning off a majority of Uniper only and holding our remaining shares at least until the end of 2017, which will be one, one and a half years, we should be able to limit additional tax burdens in relation to past restructuring measures. However, these assessments do need to be confirmed by binding tax rulings that we applied for. All in all, we regard our time line as ambitious but achievable. Nevertheless, we are also dependent on external bodies, like for example, tax authorities for binding tax rulings or the spinoff auditor nominated by local courts. I am deeply convinced that the progress achieved so far on the spinoff preparation demonstrates the extraordinary professionalism of the E.ON organization and its personnel.

In this time of significant change for E.ON, it is more important than ever to concentrate on improving and further developing our businesses. This is why I would like to update you in the next chart of progress in first and foremost, our renewable business. I am pleased to inform you that our offshore wind farm, Humber Gateway, in the British North Sea, is by now very close to be fully commissioned. We expect the final commissioning to take place in the next months. The project with an in-store capacity of 219 megawatts will be completed two months ahead of schedule and below budget. Construction began in 2012 and the first of 73 turbines came online in February of this year.

Also, and even more challenging, our Amrumbank West project, 288 megawatt offshore in the German North Sea is progressing very well and is on track to be commissioned in autumn this year. So far, close to 90% of the 80 turbines have been already installed and more than 50% of all of them have already been fully commissioned. The farm is thus connected to the grid and the production of electricity has started in April this year.

The simultaneous delivery of two large scale offshore projects in different countries in time and budget is evidence for E.ON’s exceptional offshore wind capabilities. Therefore, we intend to further strengthen our position as a leading offshore wind company.

In May, we confirmed our investment decision for the Rampion offshore wind farm in the UK. This development will have an installed capacity of even 400 megawatts and comprise 116 wind turbines. The total investment is approximately £1.3 billion. The UK Green Investment Bank has acquired a 25% stake in the project early on and does co-finance it. We do not exclude to involve a further partner in this attractive project. Commissioning is planned and secured for 2018.

In June, the development phase for the Arkona offshore wind project in the German Baltic Sea, which would have a maximum installed capacity of 385 megawatts, reached an important milestone. The nearby port of Sassnitz was selected as logistic base during construction and its future operation center. However, we there have not yet taken a final investment decision yet.

These four projects demonstrate in an exemplary way, our continued growth ambitions in the renewable area. To give you even more insight on our strong track record in the renewable business in recent years, we intend to hold a so called business deep-dive event, similar to the one we did for our distribution network some time ago. You will receive the invitation in the coming weeks.

I move now, on the next, chart five, which shows the current status of our new-build projects on the conventional side, Beresovskaya and Maasvlakte. After several years of extremely hard work, these projects are coming very close to their finalization. The principal construction work on our 800-megawatt lignite plant, Beresovskaya were completed in May, as expected. However, the commissioning works and the administration register of all these works required slightly more time than anticipated.

We have already received the permission to connect the equipment to the grid, one of the most important final milestones. Currently, we are performing the necessary comprehensive final test runs. The commissioning is now expected to be finalized in this month and to take effect in September. On a full year basis, this plant will contribute roughly 50 billion ruble to our ongoing EBITDA, pay out roughly 90% year capacity payments. The plant has very low fuel costs as a major share of the necessary lignite delivered to the plant via two 14-kilometer long open conveyor belts directly from the nearby lignite deposit.

The highly efficient 1,100 megawatt hard coal plant in Maasvlakte in the port of Rotterdam is running continuously already since March. In the meantime, testing and optimizing activities have been completed. The flexibility of this plant is state of the art and allows load changes of more than 50 megawatts per minute. The official and final commissioning procedure is underway, and we currently expect the formal commissioning to take place in October. I would like to emphasize that these two plants highlight in an exemplary way that Uniper will have quite a number of brand new assets which will require only very limited maintenance CapEx.

Furthermore, we have with the exception of Datteln 4, no more growth project going forward. Those aspects together, limited maintenance CapEx and currently limited investment needs in conventional generation are supportive for the free cash flow generation of Uniper. Therefore, I would like to take the opportunity to reassure you on the point that Uniper will have a strong development focus.

With chart six, we are moving on to regulatory and political developments over the recent months.

Although we currently regard the market environment as unattractive for investments in conventional generation, we observe continued progress in the development of the regulatory framework, albeit very slowly. Let me start with an overview of recent developments in the policy area in which we observe the most progress, the European Emissions Trading Scheme. In July, the Council, European Parliament Commission agreed on the implementation of the market stability reserve, as we had already anticipated back in March. The back-loaded allowances of 900 million tons and the still unallocated allowances of the first rounds of probably 500 million to 600 million tons will be placed into the reserve, reducing the surplus of allowances. The MSR will start in Jan 2019.

The introduction of the MSR will help to restore to a certain extent the signaling function of the CO2 price for the European climate policy. With forward prices reaching levels of around €8 per ton, coming from levels below €4 roughly two years ago, there are at least first signs that this has started to materialize. Going forward, we expect the policy discussions on the ETS will shift to a longer-term reform of the scheme and a more supportive one.

Looking at the ongoing energy reform discussions in Germany however, progress is less clear cut. In July, the government published cornerstones for the energy policy going forward. And also, the long awaited White Book on the German power market was published. Overall, the White Book is directionally supportive. We are welcoming the foreseen removal of implicit price caps and the intention to improve the remuneration for plants operating under the reserved plant ordinance. The latter should be helpful for our high efficient gas plants in Irsching in Bavaria.

We hope that improved remuneration rules will make it possible to find an economic solution that would avoid the announced closure, by allowing an adequate profitability for the plant from next year onward. However, the planned introduction of a capacity reserve instead of a full-fledged capacity market is not far reaching enough to create a sustainable environment, and to remunerate security supply in an adequate manner. From our perspective, such an instrument can only be an intermediate step. At this point, I would like to reiterate my conviction that full-fledged capacity markets, like for example in the UK, will be necessary for Germany in the coming years.

Last but not least, I would like to come to the topic of nuclear provision. The government has announced the following approach to deal with the topic of ensuring the long-term financeability of the nuclear asset retirement obligations. One element is the already running so called, stress test. On behalf of the Ministry of Economy, the German accounting firm Warth & Klein Grant Thornton is currently assessing whether the nuclear provisions have been calculated in a correct way in terms of accounting rules. This assessment is based on the existing underlying technical cost estimates. Results should be finalized by the end of September.

We are convinced that our nuclear provisions are fully adequate. There’s probably no other balance sheet item that has received more intense scrutiny from independent auditors than nuclear provisions. And international comparisons show that nuclear provisions in Germany are among the highest in the world. As the next step, the commission shall, based on the results of the stress test, develop recommendations by the end of November for potential further action. This recommendation shall take into account that the financial strength of the nuclear operators stays fully intact, a new and clear message of the government.

From our perspective, the commission shows this nuclear decommissioning, final waste storage and the numerous related issues in a comprehensive way. This new and whole process offers the big opportunity that a consensual solution between the government and nuclear operators can be achieved. We are thus absolutely open and supportive for discussions and joint solutions. However, in the last weeks we had to take note of a newly emerging uncertainty around the topic. To give you some examples, Minister Gabriel talked publicly about parents are liable for their kids or the leading German newspaper, Frankfurter Allgemeine Zeitung published an article with the headline, “government brings up the SK book for nuclear operators.” Furthermore, the government announced in this ministry that it’s currently worked on a draft law that shall foresee an almost unlimited liability for nuclear decommissioning and final waste storage charges.

The announced law sounds like making the second step before first one has been done. We think the government should wait first for the results from its own commission and the stress test before jumping to conclusions. We have to ask ourselves what all of this could potentially mean for us. We thus seek an exchange with the government with the aim to overcome concerns, clear up potential misunderstandings and have an open and constructive dialog. Once we have assessed the new situation and have more visibility, we will then come back to you.

At this point, I want to clearly reemphasize that our new strategy at its heart was never meant to be a reply to the ongoing nuclear debate in Germany. Thus, a lot of the media comments are not based on facts. We made it always very clear that we intend to set up Uniper in a way as E.ON is today that it will be perfectly capable of delivering on its nuclear asset retirement obligations with a sound investment-grade rating. But most importantly, let me assure you very clearly one thing. The board and I, together with the whole of E.ON are fully committed to our strategy. We want and will increase our business focus as a response to a fundamentally altered market environment. And our way to achieve this is to divide our portfolio into two separate companies with very distinct identities. Uniper at its core shall have an ongoing and future oriented commodity and system focus, targeting the conventional energy world for the decades to come. We see here a very long term and sustainable future as a crucial provided of security of supply.

The core of future E.ON and its future businesses shows focus on the customer in the new energy world and concentrate on renewables networks and the customer solutions, and we will deliver on that.

With these remarks, I would like to come to the final chart seven where we show you the three key takeaways of my short presentation. Firstly, the execution of the spinoff preparation is complex, lengthy but well on track. And many important milestones have been achieved. We respect the challenges from a lot of you but you can be assured that we do our utmost with hundreds of people working day and night. Secondly, we are finalizing important projects and entering the harvesting phase now on the renewable and the conventional side in the second half of this year. And finally, we are working on future, further organic growth opportunities for the future of E.ON.

Thank you for your attention. And before we come to your questions, Michael, over to you and the numbers of the first half.

Michael Sen

Yes. Thank you, Johannes for your kind introduction, and a warm welcome also from my side. I’ve been with E.ON since June 1st but it actually feels much longer than six weeks only. It was just like jumping on an accelerated high speed train. And I had to get into matters very deeply and quickly, especially concerning the transaction. I will share a few of my first impressions with you, towards the end of my presentation.

As Johannes mentioned, I’ve had a lot of touch points and experience with financial markets, interacting with the sell side and of course with investors who are the owner of our company. For me, good relations with financial markets, equity and debt alike need to build on transparency and performance. Being clear on value propositions, transparent on value drivers and following up on guidance and commitments is essential. Hence, I am looking forward to having deeper discussions with you in the future.

Let me start by giving you some color on the development of our earnings, cash flow and debt position in the first half of fiscal 2015. It goes without saying that we are still operating in a challenging business and regulatory environment, stressed oil markets, most European power markets still at over 10-year lows, more and more renewable being fed into the system. In this challenging environment, earnings in terms of EBITDA and underlying net income, both came in according to expectations. Strong cash flow and a seasonally high cash conversion stand out for the first half of fiscal 2015. And looking at our balance sheet, net debt levels improved quite a bit. In light of this, I’d characterize the first half as solid. Further, we are confirming our 2015 full year guidance even as the macro business and regulatory environment continues to evolve.

Our EBITDA for the first half came in at €4.3 billion, which is a decline of roughly €650 million or 13% versus $4.9 billion at prior year’s half. For your reference, the €4.9 billion in H1 2014 reflects the reclassification of our Spanish and Italian businesses into discontinued operations.

You may remember from our first quarter reporting that we have moved E.ON Energy sales, the sales business dealing with power and gas wholesale from E.ON Germany to Global Commodities. Hence, we have also pro forma adjusted the two segmental EBITDAs of the affected units. Global Commodities EBITDA for H1 2014 was consequently €63 million higher with the same amount negatively impacting the EBITDA of the reporting segment Germany. By year-end, this adjustment will grow to €85 million which obviously was the earnings contribution of E.ON Energy sales in 2014.

Now, let me walk you through the main parts, driving the change year over year. In fact, the EBITDA waterfall depicts a pretty representative picture of the business dynamics throughout the group at this stage of the year.

Germany delivered some earnings expansion, mainly on the back of the non-regulated part of the business. We’ve been seeing partly pricing driven improvements of the gross margin as well as weather normalization come into play. The next large positive effect which we had guided towards in March was related to new capacities coming on line across three different segments. Johannes has already elaborated on this. For 2015, we continue to expect an up to €0.3 billion positive year-over-year change from Maasvlakte, Beresovskaya and the two offshore wind projects. Yet up until now, the effect has not been material enough to make it into this reconciliation.

Equally to when we have been reporting on Q1, the overall pattern has not changed. That means we clearly experience more volatility to the downside. Therefore, it does not come as a surprise I guess also not to the Street, that we expect a significantly lower EBITDA development year over year for the full fiscal. The largest drag of about €300 million is obviously the power price and volume effect. This was also the main argument when giving our 2015 outlook.

Back then, as well as now for the first six months in essence, you can group these two effects in two buckets. First, the lower achieved prices for our nuclear and hydro generation, which account for more than half of the effect at the half. Remember, the achieved prices for power in Central Europe as well as the Nordic market are down some €6 per megawatt hour. Second, lower volumes in our nuclear generation in Sweden as well as Germany. In Germany the Grafenrheinfeld stretch-out operation is taking its toll already in H1, but in light of the final shutdown late June the volume impact from this will, of course, be more pronounced in the second half of the year.

At our E&P business, we also saw a €100 million decline compared to the first half of 2014. In March, Klaus had been guiding to a roughly €300 million to €500 million full-year contraction. Current run rate would suggest some easing. Why, because the U.S. dollar and the UK pound development year-over-year is supportive and the business has better than expected volumes. Hence, both effects partly compensate for the lower oil prices in our North Sea production and lower BAFA prices in our Russian field, Yuzhno Russkoye. Obviously, we will update you on this as we go along.

€100 million disposal effect relates to our divestiture of the Spanish generation activities, thermo, hydro and renewables to Macquarie and Wren House with the effect to the end of Q1. Whilst this just explains the fallen away earnings contribution from the regional business is already reflected in prior year’s EBITDA via the treatment as discontinued operations, the renewable and conventional generation activities have left the company with effect for the second quarter. Please be aware that due to a very high share of earnings last year and the conventional generation fleet in Spain in the isolated Q2, this effect should not be extrapolated for the full year.

In terms of what’s happening in line item other, let me point out the most important ones. As I already mentioned at the beginning of the reconciliation, there are only two segments at half year 2015 with positive earnings growth. The second one next to Germany is Global Commodities. Here we have been able to make use of our optimization potential in gas sourcing, also thanks to the more volatile winter environment. This effect represents €0.1 billion year-over-year improvement and is rather a one-off in its character.

In our non-EU segment, the improvement in our Turkish operation in Adisa was more than offset by lower earnings from Russia. There, the main driver was the weak ruble and also operational effects such as the fire in Surgutskaya power plant. All in all, this segment was down €100 million versus H1 2014.

The renewables business saw some tough comps as Q1 last year benefited from €100 million booking -- north of €100 million booking on the disposal of our offshore wind farm, Rodsand. This was partly offset in the wind, solar and other sub-segment by new capacities and favorable FX. Remember, a large share of our wind, solar and other renewables production is based in the US and in the UK.

We also saw lower earnings in our Group Management/Consolidation line driven by several effects of largely one-off character. We’ll probably not make good on this in the second half, so that the full year trend suggests we will probably be looking for a higher amount than in 2014.

And finally, we had two effects related to our Italian generation portfolio; one is related to prior-year provision releases for hydro concessions in relation to the CCGT fleet. This was not repeated and represents a low to mid-double-digit million euro effect. Of similar size is the non-extension of a special balancing power agreement with the Fiume Santo plant on the island of Sardinia which negatively impacted our H1 2015 earnings.

Now, let me shed some light on the items below the line, leading to underlying net income. Basically the contraction year-over-year in EBITDA of €650 million did not entirely drop through. Net income on an underlying basis was €1.2 billion, hence down by €300 million or 21% at the half, mainly on the back of year-over-year improvements in all line items. Again, you should be aware that all numbers on this chart reflect the pro forma adjustment for the discontinued operations in Spain and Italy. For the underlying net income, the pro forma adjustment amounted to €49 million.

So, what buffered the EBITDA drop? Lower deprecation by roughly €100 million year-over-year to €1.6 billion, stemming from a number of factors. Firstly, the assets held for sale accounting treatment of the Italian generation assets; secondly, last year’s large impairments reducing the asset base; and finally, the deconsolidation of the Spanish generation assets effective as per quarter -- the second quarter. The relief from lower E&P depreciation driven by lower volumes we had guided for with respect to the full year is not fully materializing as volumes are actually holding up quite nicely in that arena. With respect to the full year, we are becoming more optimistic, pertaining to the volume development in light of the current good run rate.

Economic interest expense improved by roughly €100 million to €800 million. You might remember that in Q1, we had explained to you the relatively high negative result, respectively the year-over-year compression. Now with respect to Q2, we are looking at a relatively low isolated quarter both are related to the same group of effects. While in Q1 record low interest rates forced us to revalue our other long-term provisions, this effect reversed in Q2.

The tax rate of around 27% is at the same level of last year, broadly in line with our indication from full-year reporting. Applied on a reduced pretax profit, this obviously leads to a lower tax number in the neighborhood of around €100 million. Hence, the reported tax rate continues to be in line with our guidance range for the full year of 25% to 30% that it is not on the lower end of the range, to some extent has to do with the diverging earnings situation at E&P. In the minority position, we usually have a strong seasonality towards the first half, in line with a strong first half seasonality of net income.

While it is nice to explain book earnings, cash usually is king. Reconciling EBITDA to operating cash flow depicts the cash conversion rate, a metric I’m used to out of the capital goods arena which probably will also play a crucial role going forward.

Non-cash effective EBITDA items during the period amounted to €1 billion. To a large extent, these consist of net provision additions. Among these, most prominent elements are provisions for CO2, needed for a fossil fleet; provisions for ROCs; pension provisions; and provisions for personnel expenses; and several other operational provisions. Most of these are part of our regular course of business, recurrent in nature and are to a similar extent, utilized on a recurrent basis, which brings me to the next point, the utilization of provisions which amounted to €1.2 billion over the period. The elements of dispositions are very similar to the constituents of the prior one, i.e. they are somewhat recurring and connected to our normal course of business.

In addition, the utilization of our nuclear provisions comes to play here, which is structurally the reason that disposition is higher. The €500 million net positive resulting from working capital movement is essentially driven by our Global Commodities business and is of somewhat seasonal character, for example due to the utilization of our gas storage and hence the connected inventory reduction. To this we do expect a compensating effect during the summer months when we refill the storages. For the first half, this is a somewhat decent cash conversion, even taking into account the seasonality at Global Commodities. Still, it is not advisable to take this as an indication for the full year 2015, as we will see some material inventory buildup in the second course of the year. However, we are currently faring well within our range of 60% to 70% cash conversion expectation for the full year.

Looking at our balance sheet, our economic net debt has been improving quite a bit in the course of the first six months. A strong cash balance, together with the proceeds from disposals has been driving the reduction of our net financial position. Economic net debt went down 4.1 billion, also having seen some significant relief coming from the pension provisions due to higher benchmark yields.

Let me run you through the key factors. As mentioned, cash balance was seasonally strong, amounting to €1.9 billion. Converting EBITDA with a rate of 99%, into an operating cash flow of €4.2 billion, covered for more than the payout on CapEx and dividends, ultimately yielding into a cash balance of €1.9 billion. For your reference, we paid our dividend of 50% per share in June, delivering on our commitment from December last year. The €0.8 billion in this reconciliation is the sum of our dividends flowing to minority shareholders and the cash component of the E.ON Group dividend. Please be reminded that disposition in cash terms is lower than the gross dividend number. This could lead to conclude that we again had an impressive pick-up of 30% -- 37% scrip dividend. Our cash effective CapEx are following the same seasonality as usual, hence they are more geared to the second half of the year. Half year CapEx was almost stable compared to last year’s level at €1.6 billion. 31% of this figure were invested in our renewables business and €1.1 billion i.e., almost 70% of the CapEx was dedicated to businesses that have been earmarked to constitute the core of future E.ON businesses.

Disposal proceeds have also helped us in the first half. The €2.4 billion of divestments is mainly, i.e., 80% attributable to our disposal of the Spanish activities, as well the Italian solar business. The bulk of the remainder is related to the sale of our remaining stake in the E.ON Energy from waste business. The €1.4 billion other, as usual summarizes the long list of different effects such as funding of the German and UK CTA, a change of Swedish nuclear provisioning, several FX effects, increased finance leases, and many more.

The pension obligation came down by €1.2 billion compared to year-end 2014. The lion’s share of this improvement can be attributed to the application of a higher discount rate for the German provisions of about 50 base points due to higher benchmark bond yields.
